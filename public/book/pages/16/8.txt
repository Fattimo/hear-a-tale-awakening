Mademoiselle&nbsp;offered Edna some chocolates in a paper bag, which she took from her pocket,&nbsp;by way of showing that she bore no ill feeling. She habitually ate chocolates&nbsp;for their sustaining quality; they contained much nutriment in small compass,&nbsp;she said. They saved her from starvation, as Madame Lebrun’s table was utterly&nbsp;impossible; and no one save so impertinent a woman as Madame Lebrun could&nbsp;think of offering such food to people and requiring them to pay for it.
“She must feel very lonely without her son,” said Edna, desiring to change&nbsp;the subject. “Her favorite son, too. It must have been quite hard to let him&nbsp;go.”
