Ma’ame Pélagie, when she saw that her sister slept, arose noiselessly and stepped&nbsp;outside upon the low-roofed narrow gallery. She did not linger there, but&nbsp;with a step that was hurried and agitated, she crossed the distance that divided&nbsp;her cabin from the ruin.
The night was not a dark one, for the sky was clear and the moon resplendent.&nbsp;But light or dark would have made no difference to Ma’ame Pélagie. It was&nbsp;not the first time she had stolen away to the ruin at night-time, when the&nbsp;whole plantation slept; but she never before had been there with a heart so&nbsp;nearly broken. She was going there for the last time to dream her dreams;&nbsp;to see the visions that hitherto had crowded her days and nights, and to bid&nbsp;them farewell.
