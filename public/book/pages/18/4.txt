She had tried to forget him, realizing the inutility of remembering.&nbsp;But the thought of him was like an obsession, ever pressing itself upon her.&nbsp;It was not that she dwelt upon details of their acquaintance, or recalled&nbsp;in any special or peculiar way his personality; it was his being, his existence,&nbsp;which dominated her thought, fading sometimes as if it would melt into the&nbsp;mist of the forgotten, reviving again with an intensity which filled her with&nbsp;an incomprehensible longing.
Edna was on her way to Madame Ratignolle’s. Their intimacy, begun at Grand&nbsp;Isle, had not declined, and they had seen each other with some frequency since&nbsp;their return to the city. The Ratignolles lived at no great distance from&nbsp;Edna’s home, on the corner of a side street, where Monsieur Ratignolle owned&nbsp;and conducted a drug store which enjoyed a steady and prosperous trade.
