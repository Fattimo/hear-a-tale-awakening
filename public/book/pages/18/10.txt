Edna felt depressed rather than soothed after leaving them. The little glimpse&nbsp;of domestic harmony which had been offered her, gave her no regret, no longing.&nbsp;It was not a condition of life which fitted her, and she could see in it but&nbsp;an appalling and hopeless ennui. She was moved by a kind of commiseration&nbsp;for Madame Ratignolle,—a pity for that colorless existence which never uplifted&nbsp;its possessor beyond the region of blind contentment, in which no moment of&nbsp;anguish ever visited her soul, in which she would never have the taste of&nbsp;life’s delirium. Edna vaguely wondered what she meant by “life’s delirium.”&nbsp;It had crossed her thought like some unsought, extraneous impression.
