Little more than a year later the transformation which the old Valmêt place&nbsp;had undergone was the talk and wonder of Côte Joyeuse. One would have looked&nbsp;in vain for the ruin; it was no longer there; neither was the log cabin. But&nbsp;out in the open, where the sun shone upon it, and the breezes blew about it,&nbsp;was a shapely structure fashioned from woods that the forests of the State&nbsp;had furnished. It rested upon a solid foundation of brick.
Upon a corner of the pleasant gallery sat Léandre smoking his afternoon cigar,&nbsp;and chatting with neighbors who had called. This was to be his pied à terre now;&nbsp;the home where his sisters and his daughter dwelt. The laughter of young people&nbsp;was heard out under the trees, and within the house where La Petite was playing&nbsp;upon the piano.
