She wanted to reach out her hand in the darkness and touch him with the sensitive
tips of her fingers upon the face or the lips. She wanted to draw close to him and
whisper against his cheek—she did not care what—as she might have done if she
had not been a respectable woman.
The stronger the impulse grew to bring herself near him, the further, in fact, did
she draw away from him. As soon as she could do so without an appearance of too
great rudeness, she rose and left him there alone.
Before she reached the house, Gouvernail had lighted a fresh cigar and ended his
apostrophe to the night.
Mrs. Baroda was greatly tempted that night to tell her husband—who was also
her friend—of this folly that had seized her. But she did not yield to the temptation.
