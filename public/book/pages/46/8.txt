She wanted to reach out her hand in the darkness and touch him with the sensitive&nbsp;tips of her fingers upon the face or the lips. She wanted to draw close to&nbsp;him and whisper against his cheek—she did not care what—as she might have&nbsp;done if she had not been a respectable woman.
The stronger the impulse grew to bring herself near him, the further, in fact,&nbsp;did she draw away from him. As soon as she could do so without an appearance&nbsp;of too great rudeness, she rose and left him there alone.
Before she reached the house, Gouvernail had lighted a fresh cigar and ended&nbsp;his apostrophe to the night.
Mrs. Baroda was greatly tempted that night to tell her husband—who was also&nbsp;her friend—of this folly that had seized her. But she did not yield to the&nbsp;temptation.
