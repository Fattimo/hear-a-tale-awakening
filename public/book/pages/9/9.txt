Edna was what she herself called very fond of music. Musical strains, well&nbsp;rendered, had a way of evoking pictures in her mind. She sometimes liked to&nbsp;sit in the room of mornings when Madame Ratignolle played or practiced. One&nbsp;piece which that lady played Edna had entitled “Solitude.” It was a short,&nbsp;plaintive, minor strain. The name of the piece was something else, but she&nbsp;called it “Solitude.” When she heard it there came before her imagination&nbsp;the figure of a man standing beside a desolate rock on the seashore. He was&nbsp;naked. His attitude was one of hopeless resignation as he looked toward a&nbsp;distant bird winging its flight away from him.
