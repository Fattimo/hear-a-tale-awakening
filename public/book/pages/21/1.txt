Some people contended that the reason Mademoiselle Reisz always chose apartments&nbsp;up under the roof was to discourage the approach of beggars, peddlars and&nbsp;callers. There were plenty of windows in her little front room. They were&nbsp;for the most part dingy, but as they were nearly always open it did not make&nbsp;so much difference. They often admitted into the room a good deal of smoke&nbsp;and soot; but at the same time all the light and air that there was came through&nbsp;them. From her windows could be seen the crescent of the river, the masts&nbsp;of ships and the big chimneys of the Mississippi steamers. A magnificent piano&nbsp;crowded the apartment. In the next room she slept, and in the third and last&nbsp;she harbored a gasoline stove on which she cooked her meals when disinclined&nbsp;to descend to the neighboring restaurant.
