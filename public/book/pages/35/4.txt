She said possibly she might, and he negotiated with her for some Parisian&nbsp;studies to reach him in time for the holiday trade in December.
Robert did not come that day. She was keenly disappointed. He did not come&nbsp;the following day, nor the next. Each morning she awoke with hope, and each&nbsp;night she was a prey to despondency. She was tempted to seek him out. But&nbsp;far from yielding to the impulse, she avoided any occasion which might throw&nbsp;her in his way. She did not go to Mademoiselle Reisz’s nor pass by Madame&nbsp;Lebrun’s, as she might have done if he had still been in Mexico.
When Arobin, one night, urged her to drive with him, she went—out to the lake,&nbsp;on the Shell Road. His horses were full of mettle, and even a little unmanageable.&nbsp;She liked the rapid gait at which they spun along, and the quick, sharp sound&nbsp;of the horses’ hoofs on the hard road.
