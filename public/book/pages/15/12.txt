“Don’t part from me in any ill humor. I never knew you to be out of patience&nbsp;with me before.”
“I don’t want to part in any ill humor,” she said. “But can’t you understand?&nbsp;I’ve grown used to seeing you, to having you with me all the time, and your&nbsp;action seems unfriendly, even unkind. You don’t even offer an excuse for it.&nbsp;Why, I was planning to be together, thinking of how pleasant it would be to&nbsp;see you in the city next winter.”
“So was I,” he blurted. “Perhaps that’s the—” He stood up suddenly and held&nbsp;out his hand. “Good-by, my dear Mrs. Pontellier; good-by. You won’t—I hope&nbsp;you won’t completely forget me.” She clung to his hand, striving to detain&nbsp;him.
“Write to me when you get there, won’t you, Robert?” she entreated.
“I will, thank you. Good-by.”
