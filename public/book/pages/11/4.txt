Edna began to feel like one who awakens gradually out of a dream, a delicious,&nbsp;grotesque, impossible dream, to feel again the realities pressing into her&nbsp;soul. The physical need for sleep began to overtake her; the exuberance which&nbsp;had sustained and exalted her spirit left her helpless and yielding to the&nbsp;conditions which crowded her in.
The stillest hour of the night had come, the hour before dawn, when the world&nbsp;seems to hold its breath. The moon hung low, and had turned from silver to&nbsp;copper in the sleeping sky. The old owl no longer hooted, and the water-oaks&nbsp;had ceased to moan as they bent their heads.
Edna arose, cramped from lying so long and still in the hammock. She tottered&nbsp;up the steps, clutching feebly at the post before passing into the house.
