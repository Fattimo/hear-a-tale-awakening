Mrs. Pontellier was not a woman given to confidences, a characteristic hitherto&nbsp;contrary to her nature. Even as a child she had lived her own small life all&nbsp;within herself. At a very early period she had apprehended instinctively the&nbsp;dual life—that outward existence which conforms, the inward life which questions.
That summer at Grand Isle she began to loosen a little the mantle of reserve&nbsp;that had always enveloped her. There may have been—there must have been—influences,&nbsp;both subtle and apparent, working in their several ways to induce her to do&nbsp;this; but the most obvious was the influence of Adèle Ratignolle. The excessive&nbsp;physical charm of the Creole had first attracted her, for Edna had a sensuous&nbsp;susceptibility to beauty.
