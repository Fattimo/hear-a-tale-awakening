“It seems to me the utmost folly for a woman at the head of a household, and&nbsp;the mother of children, to spend in an atelier days which would be better&nbsp;employed contriving for the comfort of her family.”
“I feel like painting,” answered Edna. “Perhaps I shan’t always feel like&nbsp;it.”
“Then in God’s name paint! but don’t let the family go to the devil. There’s&nbsp;Madame Ratignolle; because she keeps up her music, she doesn’t let everything&nbsp;else go to chaos. And she’s more of a musician than you are a painter.”
“She isn’t a musician, and I’m not a painter. It isn’t on account of painting&nbsp;that I let things go.”
“On account of what, then?”
“Oh! I don’t know. Let me alone; you bother me.”
It sometimes entered Mr. Pontellier’s mind to wonder if his wife were not&nbsp;growing a little unbalanced mentally. He could see plainly that she was not&nbsp;herself.
