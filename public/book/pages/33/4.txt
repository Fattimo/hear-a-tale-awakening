Edna.
“Don’t neglect me,” entreated Madame Ratignolle; “and don’t mind what I said&nbsp;about Arobin, or having some one to stay with you.”
“Of course not,” Edna laughed. “You may say anything you like to me.” They&nbsp;kissed each other good-by. Madame Ratignolle had not far to go, and Edna stood&nbsp;on the porch a while watching her walk down the street.
Then in the afternoon Mrs. Merriman and Mrs. Highcamp had made their “party&nbsp;call.” Edna felt that they might have dispensed with the formality. They had&nbsp;also come to invite her to play vingt-et-un one evening at Mrs. Merriman’s.&nbsp;She was asked to go early, to dinner, and Mr. Merriman or Mr. Arobin would&nbsp;take her home. Edna accepted in a half-hearted way. She sometimes felt very&nbsp;tired of Mrs. Highcamp and Mrs. Merriman.
Late in the afternoon she sought refuge with Mademoiselle Reisz, and stayed&nbsp;there alone, waiting for her, feeling a kind of repose
